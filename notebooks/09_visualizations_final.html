from IPython.display import Image, display
from pathlib import Path

FIGS = Path('../output/figures')

Figure 1: BTM Adoption Trajectory (2015–2023)#

Caption: Behind-the-meter residential solar capacity grew 9x across California’s three largest utilities (2015–2023), with Southern California Edison leading. The sharp 2023 plateau reflects the shift from NEM 2.0 to the less-generous Net Billing Tariff (effective April 15, 2023).

display(Image(FIGS / 'adoption_trajectory.png', width=900))

Figure 2: Adoption Density Heatmap (2015–2023)#

Caption: BTM solar adoption concentrated in high-solar-potential suburban ZIP codes, particularly in Southern California (Riverside, Kern, San Bernardino counties). Darker reds indicate higher cumulative capacity. Geographic clustering is visible and persistent across the 9-year period.

display(Image(FIGS / 'adoption_density_heatmap.png', width=900))

Figure 3: Duck Curve Evolution — 2015 vs. 2023#

Caption: The California grid’s net-load curve shifted dramatically between 2015 and 2023. Utility-scale solar generation grew from ~2 GW to ~13 GW at peak, pushing midday net demand to near-zero. The evening demand ramp (4–8 PM) steepened, creating operational challenges. This transition is known as the ‘duck-to-canyon’ evolution.

display(Image(FIGS / 'duck_curve_evolution.png', width=900))

Figure 4: Hourly Load Profiles — 2015, 2018, 2022#

Caption: CAISO average daily load profiles (2015, 2018, 2022) show solar’s growing contribution. By 2022, midday solar generation peaks at 13 GW, reducing net demand to historic lows. The evening ramp (4–8 PM) became steeper over time as this solar contribution was withdrawn.

display(Image(FIGS / 'duck_curve_hourly_profiles.png', width=900))

Figure 5: Grid Stress — Net-Load Ramp Magnitude (2015–2023)#

Caption: Net-load ramp magnitude (mean hourly change in demand) increased through 2023, with pronounced summer peaks reflecting seasonal demand volatility. This metric captures the operational challenge of matching supply to sharp evening demand increases. Orange shading marks June–September summer periods.

display(Image(FIGS / 'grid_stress_ramp_magnitude.png', width=900))

Figure 6: Moran’s I Scatterplot — Spatial Autocorrelation Diagnostic#

Caption: Regression residuals show weak spatial clustering (Moran’s I ≈ 0.10, p < 0.05), indicating that ZIP-level adoption patterns do not strongly predict ZIP-specific ramp behavior. Grid stress is a system-wide phenomenon insensitive to geographic concentration of solar installations.

display(Image(FIGS / 'morans_scatter.png', width=900))

Limitations & Data Granularity#

This analysis is system-level in scope. CAISO metrics (net load, curtailment, wholesale prices) are aggregate California-wide values — they do not vary by ZIP code or utility territory. This is a fundamental constraint of publicly available grid operations data.

Why ZIP-level adoption doesn’t predict ZIP-level grid stress:

  1. Aggregate effect dominates: When solar ramps down at sunset across all of California, CAISO sees a single system-level event. Whether solar is concentrated in Riverside or spread across 100 ZIPs, the system ramp is the same.

  2. Utility-scale solar drives the curve: The duck-curve deepening is primarily driven by utility-scale solar (Mojave Desert, Central Valley projects), not distributed BTM solar.

  3. Data granularity mismatch: System-level CAISO metrics are invariant across all ZIPs within the CAISO interconnection — the same ramp magnitude is assigned to every ZIP.

To test local effects, we would need:

  • Feeder-level voltage and frequency data (not publicly available)

  • Distribution-network operator data (requires utility data-sharing agreements)

  • Customer-level interval metering data (requires Green Button access)

See limitations.md for a full discussion.
